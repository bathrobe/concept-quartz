#### Summary
In this Berggruen Institute lecture, Benjamin Bratton explores how planetary-scale computation is reshaping our understanding of intelligence, governance, and humanity's relationship with Earth through the lens of philosophy and technological evolution.

#### Main Points
- Computation should be understood as a planetary infrastructure that humans discovered as much as invented, with implications far beyond simple data processing.
- Modern artificial intelligence represents a new form of intelligence that requires new philosophical frameworks beyond traditional anthropocentric models.
- The fragmentation of planetary computation into "hemispherical stacks" reflects and shapes contemporary geopolitics.

#### Bullet Summary
- Early computation, like the Antikythera mechanism, was fundamentally about orienting humans in relation to planetary conditions.
- Climate science itself is an accomplishment of planetary computation, enabling understanding of human impact on Earth systems.
- Traditional Western philosophical approaches to AI often mistakenly focus on human-like intelligence rather than new forms of intelligence.
- The "stack" represents an accidental megastructure of planetary computation with distinct layers from Earth to user.
- Geopolitical tensions increasingly center on computational capabilities, from chip manufacturing to data sovereignty.
- The evolution of artificial intelligence should be understood as a planetary phenomenon rather than a national one.
- Current technological capabilities often exceed our conceptual frameworks for understanding them.
- Proper governance of planetary computation requires moving beyond traditional state-centric models.
- The future of AI will likely be shaped by both competition and cooperation between major powers.
- Understanding planetary computation requires reconciling scientific and cultural cosmologies.
