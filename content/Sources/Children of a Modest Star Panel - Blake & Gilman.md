#### Summary
A Carnegie Endowment panel discussion explores how humanity's relationship with Earth requires new planetary-scale governance approaches, as discussed in the book "Children of a Modest Star" by Jonathan Blake and Nils Gilman.

#### Main Points
- The current international system of sovereign nation-states is fundamentally unable to address planetary challenges like climate change and pandemics because these phenomena don't respect borders.
- The authors propose a multi-scalar governance approach where different challenges are addressed at appropriate levels: planetary, national, and local.
- The European Union serves as an imperfect but instructive model for moving beyond traditional sovereignty, though its focus on economic integration has limited its effectiveness on planetary issues.

#### Bullet Summary
- The book was inspired by observing how national responses to COVID-19 proved inadequate for addressing a planetary-scale challenge.
- The authors identify three historical "decenterings" of human thought: Copernican astronomy, Darwinian evolution, and now planetary thinking.
- Earth system science shows humans are embedded within, not separate from, planetary systems.
- Current international institutions were designed to manage human relations between states, not planetary phenomena.
- The authors propose an "IPCC with teeth" that could set binding planetary carbon limits.
- Local climate adaptation should be handled through "translocal networked governance" connecting regions with similar challenges.
- The European Union demonstrates both the possibilities and limitations of moving beyond traditional state sovereignty.
- Technology transfer and distribution, particularly for green technologies, needs reform beyond current profit-driven models.
- Questions of economic growth, capitalism, and development remain central challenges for planetary governance.
- Building planetary identity and institutions may require major disruption rather than gradual change.

#### Interesting Details
- The concept of sovereignty originated as a way for kings to protect themselves from other kings, making it poorly suited for addressing planetary challenges.
- Jakarta, Indonesia and Rotterdam, Netherlands are collaborating on urban water management due to similar sea-level challenges, despite their very different contexts.
- The US failure to distribute COVID vaccines globally is seen as a cautionary tale for managing green technology transfer.
- The EU's Erasmus program was designed to build European identity through educational exchanges.
