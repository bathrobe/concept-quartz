Advanced AI systems may provide state-level capabilities without requiring traditional state infrastructure or scale.

- Computational systems could match or exceed traditional state functions in areas like research, analysis, and planning.
- This enables new forms of governance that can leverage AI to achieve state-like capabilities with minimal physical infrastructure.
- The accessibility of such systems could fundamentally alter the minimum viable scale for effective governance entities.
- Network states and other new governance forms may need to develop frameworks for managing and deploying such capabilities responsibly.

Implications:
- Small organizations could potentially wield capabilities previously reserved for nation-states
- The "minimum viable state" could shrink dramatically as AI systems replace traditional bureaucracies
- Power projection may become increasingly disconnected from physical territory or population size
- New governance frameworks needed to manage entities with state-like capabilities but non-state structures

Risks:
- Potential for rapid proliferation of entities with state-level capabilities
- Difficulty in establishing accountability for synthetic nations
- Challenge of maintaining democratic control over AI-powered governance systems
- Unclear boundaries between corporate, state, and synthetic national powers
